Key Takeaway

The difference between long-term and short-term capital gains tax rates is one of the most important concepts in investing. Short-term capital gains (assets held 1 year or less) are taxed at your ordinary income tax rate — up to 37% in 2025. Long-term capital gains (assets held more than 1 year) are taxed at preferential rates — 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your taxable income. This rate difference can save you thousands of dollars on every investment sale. Simply holding an asset for at least one year and one day before selling can cut your tax rate by half or more.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains: The Basics

When you sell a capital asset — stocks, bonds, real estate, cryptocurrency, or other investments — the profit is called a capital gain. The IRS divides capital gains into two categories based on how long you held the asset before selling:

  • Short-term capital gains: Assets held for 1 year or less. Taxed at your ordinary income tax rate (10% to 37% in 2025).
  • Long-term capital gains: Assets held for more than 1 year. Taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20% in 2025).

The holding period is measured from the day after you acquire the asset to the day you sell it. If you buy shares on June 1, 2025, and sell them on June 2, 2026, you have held them for more than one year and qualify for long-term treatment. If you sell on June 1, 2026, the gain is short-term.

Visual Comparison: The Tax Impact of One Extra Day

The difference between short-term and long-term rates is dramatic. Consider this example:

Sarah is a single filer with a salary of $90,000 in 2025. She sells $20,000 worth of stock that she bought for $10,000, generating a $10,000 gain. If she held the stock for 11 months, the gain is short-term and taxed at 22% — she owes $2,200. If she waited just one more month (12 months + 1 day), the gain is long-term and taxed at 15% — she owes $1,500. The one-month wait saves her $700.

For higher-income investors, the savings are even more dramatic. A married couple earning $400,000 who sells cryptocurrency for a $50,000 gain would pay $18,500 (37% short-term rate) versus $10,000 (20% long-term rate) — a savings of $8,500.

The Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

High-income investors may also owe the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax on the lesser of their net investment income or the amount by which their modified AGI exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (joint). This surtax applies to both short-term and long-term capital gains, increasing the total tax rate to 23.8% for long-term gains (20% + 3.8%) and up to 40.8% for short-term gains (37% + 3.8%) for top earners.

The 0% Long-Term Capital Gains Bracket

One of the most valuable tax planning tools is the 0% long-term capital gains bracket. For 2025, single filers with taxable income up to $48,350 and married joint filers with taxable income up to $96,700 pay no tax on long-term capital gains. This creates powerful planning opportunities:

  • Tax-gain harvesting: In years when your income is low, you can intentionally realize capital gains up to the 0% bracket limit and pay no tax. You then reset your cost basis higher, reducing future taxable gains.
  • Retirement planning: Retirees with low taxable income can sell appreciated investments and pay 0% capital gains tax. This is a key strategy for managing taxable income in retirement.
  • Gifting appreciated assets to family: If you gift appreciated assets to a family member in the 0% bracket, they can sell them and pay no capital gains tax (subject to the kiddie tax rules for children).

Tax-Gain Harvesting

In a low-income year, sell appreciated investments up to the 0% bracket limit. Repurchase immediately (no wash-sale concerns for gains) and reset your cost basis higher, reducing future tax liability.

Charitable Donations

Donate appreciated long-term assets directly to charity. You avoid capital gains tax entirely and can deduct the full fair market value as a charitable contribution (subject to AGI limits).

Step-Up in Basis at Death

Holding appreciated assets until death eliminates capital gains tax entirely — your heirs receive a step-up in basis to the date-of-death value. The gains accumulated during your lifetime are never taxed.

Capital Gains and Different Asset Types

While the basic short-term vs. long-term distinction applies to most assets, some have special rules:

  • Cryptocurrency: Treated as property by the IRS. Short-term gains are taxed at ordinary rates; long-term gains at preferential rates. Crypto held for more than one year qualifies for long-term treatment.
  • Real estate: Gains from selling your primary residence (up to $250,000 for single filers, $500,000 for joint filers) may be excluded if you meet the ownership and use tests. Real estate held for investment is subject to capital gains rates plus depreciation recapture (25%).
  • Collectibles: Gains from collectibles (art, antiques, coins, precious metals) are taxed at a maximum rate of 28% regardless of holding period — higher than the standard long-term rate for most investors.
  • Small business stock (QSBS): Gains from qualified small business stock held for more than 5 years may be excluded from tax up to $10 million or 10 times the basis (Section 1202).

Strategies to Optimize Capital Gains Taxes

Here are practical strategies to minimize your capital gains tax burden:

1

Hold Assets for Over One Year

The single most effective strategy. Simply waiting 366+ days before selling cuts your tax rate from ordinary income rates to preferential long-term rates — potentially saving 10–17%.

2

Harvest Losses Strategically

Sell losing investments to offset gains. Losses first offset gains of the same type, then offset gains of the other type, then up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year.

3

Time Your Sales to Low-Income Years

If you have flexibility, realize gains in years when your income is in the 0% or 15% long-term capital gains bracket. Between jobs, sabbatical years, or retirement are ideal.

4

Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Hold investments in Roth IRAs (tax-free growth), traditional IRAs (tax-deferred), or 401(k)s. Inside these accounts, the short-term vs. long-term distinction does not matter.

5

Donate Appreciated Assets

Donate long-term appreciated assets to charity instead of cash. You avoid capital gains tax and deduct the full fair market value as a charitable contribution.

6

Use Specific Identification

When selling a portion of your holdings, use spec ID to select shares with the highest cost basis (lowest gain). This minimizes your current tax liability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I pay capital gains tax if I sell at a loss?

No. If you sell an asset for less than you paid, you have a capital loss, not a gain. Losses can offset other gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year. Losses carry forward indefinitely.

How does the holding period work for gifted assets?

When you receive a gift, your holding period includes the time the original owner held the asset. This is called "tacking" the holding period. If your parents held a stock for 3 years and gift it to you, you are treated as having held it for 3+ years for capital gains purposes.

How does the holding period work for inherited assets?

Inherited assets are always treated as long-term regardless of how long the decedent held them or how long you hold them before selling. Additionally, your basis is stepped up to the date-of-death value.

Can I use short-term losses to offset long-term gains?

No. The IRS requires you to net short-term losses against short-term gains first, and long-term losses against long-term gains first. Only after netting within each category can you use excess losses of one type to offset gains of the other type.

Do capital gains affect my Social Security taxation?

Yes. Capital gains are included in your provisional income, which determines what percentage of your Social Security benefits are taxable. Realizing large capital gains can push you over the threshold and make up to 85% of your benefits taxable.

What is the capital gains rate for corporate stock?

The same rates apply to corporate stock as to other assets. Short-term: ordinary income rates. Long-term: 0%, 15%, or 20% plus the 3.8% NIIT for high earners.

Do capital gains count as income for the premium tax credit?

Yes. Capital gains are included in your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI), which determines your eligibility for the Premium Tax Credit on health insurance. Large capital gains could reduce or eliminate your subsidy.

How do I report capital gains on my tax return?

Report capital gains and losses on Form 8949 (Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets) and transfer the totals to Schedule D (Capital Gains and Losses). Your broker provides Form 1099-B with the transaction details.

The Bottom Line

The difference between short-term and long-term capital gains rates is one of the most valuable tax planning opportunities available to investors. Short-term gains are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%), while long-term gains are taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%). Holding an asset for just one year and one day can reduce your tax rate by half or more. Combine long-term holding with strategic tax-loss harvesting, tax-gain harvesting in low-income years, and donating appreciated assets to charity to minimize your lifetime capital gains tax burden. Understanding and applying these rules can save you tens of thousands of dollars over your investing lifetime.
