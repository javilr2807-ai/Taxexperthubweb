Key Takeaway

Dividends are taxed at different rates depending on whether they are classified as qualified or ordinary. Qualified dividends are taxed at long-term capital gains rates — 0%, 15%, or 20% — which are significantly lower than ordinary income rates. Ordinary (non-qualified) dividends are taxed at your marginal income tax rate, up to 37% in 2025. For a dividend to be qualified, it must be paid by a U.S. corporation or qualifying foreign corporation, and you must have held the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period surrounding the ex-dividend date.

Qualified vs. Ordinary Dividends: The Key Distinction

The IRS divides dividends into two categories for tax purposes. The distinction is critical because the tax rates are dramatically different:

  • Qualified dividends: Taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% in 2025). To qualify, the dividend must meet specific holding period and issuer requirements.
  • Ordinary (non-qualified) dividends: Taxed at your ordinary income tax rate (10% to 37% in 2025). These include dividends from REITs, MLPs, certain foreign corporations, and dividends that do not meet the holding period requirement.

Most dividends paid by U.S. corporations — including the vast majority of stocks in the S&P 500 — are qualified dividends. Your broker reports the breakdown between qualified and ordinary dividends on Form 1099-DIV, which you receive each tax year. The Holding Period Requirement

For a dividend to be qualified, you must meet a specific holding period requirement. You must have held the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period that begins 60 days before the ex-dividend date. The ex-dividend date is the date on which the stock trades without the dividend — typically one business day before the record date.

This requirement prevents investors from buying a stock just before the dividend date, collecting the dividend at a preferential rate, and then selling immediately. If you fail the holding period test, the dividend is treated as ordinary income. The holding period is also affected by certain transactions that reduce your risk of loss — such as buying put options or selling call options — which can suspend the holding period.

Example: Meeting the Holding Period

Suppose Company XYZ has an ex-dividend date of June 10, 2025. You buy 100 shares on May 1, 2025. The 121-day period runs from April 11 (60 days before June 10) to August 9 (60 days after June 10). You must hold the shares for more than 60 days during this period. Since you bought on May 1 and held through June 10 (41 days) plus the next 20 days, you would have held for 61 days — meeting the requirement. Your dividend is qualified.

Dividend Tax Rates by Income Level

For 2025, the qualified dividend tax rates are tied to the long-term capital gains brackets:

  • 0% rate: Single filers with taxable income up to $48,350; married joint filers up to $96,700; head of household up to $64,775.
  • 15% rate: Single filers with taxable income $48,351–$533,400; married joint filers $96,701–$633,750; head of household $64,776–$566,700.
  • 20% rate: Single filers with taxable income above $533,400; married joint filers above $633,750; head of household above $566,700.

Note that the 15% bracket covers a very wide income range — most middle-income and upper-middle-income investors with qualified dividends pay the 15% rate. The 20% rate and the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) apply only to high-income investors.

Strategies to Minimize Dividend Taxes

Here are practical strategies to reduce the tax impact of your dividend income:

Hold Dividend Stocks in Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Dividend-paying stocks and funds are best held in IRAs, 401(k)s, or HSAs, where dividends grow tax-deferred or tax-free. Taxable accounts are better for growth stocks that pay little or no dividends.

Focus on Qualified Dividends

When investing in taxable accounts, prioritize stocks and funds that pay qualified dividends. REITs, MLPs, and high-yield bond funds pay mostly ordinary dividends taxed at higher rates.

Meet the Holding Period

Hold dividend stocks for at least 61 days around the ex-dividend date to qualify for the lower qualified dividend rate. Avoid selling dividend stocks within 60 days of purchase.

Consider Tax-Managed Funds

Some mutual funds and ETFs are specifically managed to minimize taxable distributions. Tax-managed funds use strategies like selective trading and offsetting gains with losses to reduce dividend and capital gain payouts.

Use Municipal Bonds for Income

For taxable accounts, municipal bonds and municipal bond funds provide tax-free income at the federal level, making them more attractive than taxable bond funds for high-bracket investors.

Plan Around the NIIT Threshold

If your MAGI exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (joint), the 3.8% NIIT applies to the lesser of your net investment income or the excess over the threshold. Consider strategies to keep MAGI below these thresholds.

Dividend Reinvestment and Tax Liability

Many investors participate in dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs), where dividends are automatically used to purchase additional shares. It is important to understand that dividend reinvestment does not avoid taxes. You still owe tax on the dividends in the year they are paid, even if you reinvest them. The reinvestment simply means you use your after-tax dividends to buy more shares.

Each reinvestment creates a new tax lot with its own cost basis and holding period. This means that if you reinvest dividends monthly in a DRIP, you may have dozens of tax lots to track when you eventually sell. Most brokers handle this tracking automatically and report it on Form 1099-B. However, it can complicate tax-loss harvesting because it is harder to identify specific lots with losses.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are stock dividends taxable?

Stock dividends (additional shares of the same company) are generally not taxable when received. However, they adjust your cost basis — the total basis remains the same but is spread across more shares. When you sell, the lower per-share basis may result in a larger capital gain.

Do I pay tax on dividends in my IRA?

No, not in the year received. Dividends in a traditional IRA grow tax-deferred — you pay tax when you withdraw. Dividends in a Roth IRA grow tax-free — you pay no tax on qualified withdrawals. Dividends in a taxable account are taxed in the year received.

How are foreign dividends taxed?

Foreign dividends are generally taxable. Many foreign corporations' dividends qualify for the lower qualified dividend rate if the corporation meets certain criteria. You may also be eligible for the foreign tax credit on taxes paid to foreign governments.

What is the difference between a dividend and a capital gain distribution?

A dividend is a distribution of a company's profits to shareholders. A capital gain distribution is paid by a mutual fund or ETF when the fund manager sells securities for a profit. Capital gain distributions are taxable and are reported on Form 1099-DIV.

Do I pay NIIT on dividends?

Yes. The 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax applies to dividend income (both qualified and ordinary) if your modified AGI exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (joint).

Can I use capital losses to offset dividend income?

No. Capital losses offset capital gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year, but dividend income is not considered "ordinary income" for this purpose. Dividends are classified as investment income and cannot be offset by capital losses beyond the $3,000 limit.

How do I report dividends on my tax return?

Report ordinary dividends on Schedule B (Form 1040) if they exceed $1,500. Transfer the total to Form 1040, Line 3b. Qualified dividends are reported on Form 1040, Line 3a. Most tax software handles this automatically from your Form 1099-DIV.

Are dividends from money market funds taxed as dividends or interest?

Money market fund "dividends" are typically taxed as ordinary interest income, not as qualified dividends. They are reported on Form 1099-INT or Form 1099-DIV depending on the fund structure, but in either case, they are taxed at ordinary income rates.

The Bottom Line

Dividend taxation depends entirely on whether the dividend is qualified (taxed at 0–20%) or ordinary (taxed at your marginal rate up to 37%). The difference is dramatic — a high-income investor could pay 20% on qualified dividends versus 37% on ordinary dividends. To maximize tax efficiency, hold dividend-paying stocks in tax-advantaged accounts, meet the 61-day holding period to qualify for lower rates, and prioritize investments that pay qualified dividends in your taxable accounts. Understanding these rules can save you thousands of dollars in taxes each year on your dividend income.
