Key Takeaway

Tax-loss harvesting is a strategy where you sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains from other investments, reducing your overall tax liability. If your losses exceed your gains, you can deduct up to $3,000 per year ($1,500 if married filing separately) against ordinary income. Unused losses carry forward indefinitely to future tax years. This strategy is particularly valuable in volatile markets and for investors with substantial capital gains from other assets, including cryptocurrency and real estate.

What Is Tax-Loss Harvesting?

Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of selling securities that have declined in value to realize a capital loss. This loss can then be used to offset capital gains from other investments, reducing your taxable income. If your capital losses exceed your capital gains, you can use up to $3,000 of the excess loss to offset ordinary income each year.

The strategy is called "harvesting" because you intentionally realize losses to capture their tax benefit. Without harvesting, the loss remains "unrealized" — it exists on paper but has no tax impact. By selling the losing investment, you convert the paper loss into a realized loss that you can use on your tax return.

How Tax-Loss Harvesting Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Here is a concrete example to illustrate how tax-loss harvesting works in practice:

Maria has a portfolio with three investments:

  • Stock A: Bought for $10,000, now worth $14,000 (unrealized gain of $4,000)
  • Stock B: Bought for $10,000, now worth $7,000 (unrealized loss of $3,000)
  • Stock C: Bought for $10,000, now worth $6,000 (unrealized loss of $4,000)

Maria decides to harvest her losses. She sells Stock B and Stock C, realizing total losses of $7,000. She then sells Stock A, realizing a gain of $4,000. The $4,000 gain is offset by $4,000 of the losses, leaving her with a net loss of $3,000. She can deduct this $3,000 against her ordinary income, saving approximately $660 in taxes (assuming a 22% bracket). She can carry forward the remaining unused loss of $0 (since she used $4,000 to offset the gain and $3,000 against income, using all $7,000).

The Wash-Sale Rule

The wash-sale rule (IRC Section 1091) prevents you from claiming a loss if you buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. If you trigger the wash-sale rule, the loss is disallowed and added to the cost basis of the replacement shares. This rule applies to stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and options — but not to cryptocurrency (as of 2025, the IRS has not classified crypto as securities for wash-sale purposes). Always wait at least 31 days before repurchasing the same or substantially identical investment.

Tax-Loss Harvesting for Cryptocurrency

Cryptocurrency offers a unique advantage for tax-loss harvesting: the wash-sale rule does not apply to cryptocurrency (as of 2025). This means you can sell a crypto asset at a loss and immediately repurchase the same asset without waiting 31 days. This is a significant advantage over stocks and ETFs, where the wash-sale rule limits harvesting opportunities.

However, crypto tax-loss harvesting requires careful tracking of your cost basis. The IRS allows you to use specific identification (spec ID) to choose which units you are selling, which is essential for maximizing the tax benefit. If you use FIFO (first-in, first-out), you may not be able to target the lots with the largest losses. Most crypto tax software (CoinTracker, Koinly, CryptoTrader.Tax) supports spec ID identification for optimal tax-loss harvesting.

Strategic Uses of Tax-Loss Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting can be used in several strategic ways beyond simply offsetting gains:

1

Offset Short-Term Gains

Short-term capital gains (assets held less than 1 year) are taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37%. Harvested losses first offset short-term gains, which provides the greatest tax benefit.

2

Offset Long-Term Gains

If you have more losses than short-term gains, the remaining losses offset long-term gains (taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%). This is valuable but less beneficial than offsetting short-term gains.

3

Deduct Against Ordinary Income

Up to $3,000 of net capital losses can be deducted against ordinary income each year. This is the most valuable use of losses because ordinary income rates are higher than capital gains rates for most taxpayers.

4

Carry Forward Losses

Unused capital losses carry forward indefinitely to future tax years. This is particularly useful in years when you have no gains — the losses are preserved for when you sell appreciated assets.

5

Rebalance Without Tax Cost

Harvesting losses from declining assets can fund a portfolio rebalance. You sell losers to realize losses, then use the proceeds to buy other assets that align with your target allocation.

6

Tax-Gain Harvesting Combo

In years when your income is low (0% capital gains bracket), you can intentionally realize gains up to the bracket limit and offset them with harvested losses from other years.

Limitations and Risks

While tax-loss harvesting is a powerful strategy, it has limitations and risks:

  • $3,000 deduction limit. You can only deduct $3,000 of net capital losses against ordinary income per year ($1,500 if MFS). Excess losses carry forward, but you do not get the full benefit immediately.
  • Wash-sale rules for securities. Violating the wash-sale rule disallows the loss and can create record-keeping headaches. Track your purchases carefully around tax-loss harvesting sales.
  • Short-term vs. long-term classification. Losses must be netted against gains of the same type first. Short-term losses must be used against short-term gains before offsetting long-term gains. This can limit the strategic value of some harvested losses.
  • Portfolio disruption. Selling a losing investment to realize the loss means you are no longer invested in that asset. You need a replacement strategy — either buying a similar (not substantially identical) asset or accepting the allocation change.
  • Transaction costs. Trading commissions are now largely zero at major brokerages, but bid-ask spreads and market impact can still affect the cost-effectiveness of harvesting smaller losses.

The Cost-Benefit Analysis

Tax-loss harvesting is most beneficial when losses are large relative to the transaction costs and the holding period is short enough that the wash-sale rule is not a significant constraint. For losses under $1,000, the tax savings may not justify the effort, especially for stocks subject to wash-sale rules. For losses of $3,000 or more, the potential tax savings of $660+ (at 22% bracket) make harvesting worthwhile for most investors.

Tax-Loss Harvesting vs. Tax-Gain Harvesting

Tax-gain harvesting is the opposite strategy: intentionally realizing capital gains in years when your income is low enough that you fall into the 0% long-term capital gains bracket. For 2025, the 0% long-term capital gains bracket applies to taxable income up to $48,350 for single filers and $96,700 for married filing jointly.

By combining tax-loss harvesting with tax-gain harvesting, you can manage your portfolio's tax liability over multiple years. Sell losing positions to generate losses that offset gains, and in low-income years, realize gains up to the 0% bracket limit. This strategy, sometimes called tax-alpha harvesting, can add 0.5% to 1.5% to your annual after-tax returns.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does tax-loss harvesting always make sense?

No. Tax-loss harvesting is most valuable when you have capital gains to offset or when you can use the $3,000 ordinary income deduction. For small losses, the transaction costs and complexity may outweigh the tax savings.

Can I harvest losses in my retirement accounts?

No. Losses realized inside tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, 401(k), HSA) do not create tax benefits because gains inside these accounts are also not taxed. Tax-loss harvesting only works in taxable brokerage accounts.

What happens to harvested losses if I die?

When you pass away, your heirs receive a step-up in basis on inherited assets. Any unrealized losses in your portfolio are permanently lost — they cannot be transferred to your heirs or used on your final tax return.

Can I harvest losses on mutual funds?

Yes, but wash-sale rules apply. If you sell a mutual fund at a loss and buy shares in a "substantially identical" fund within 30 days, the loss is disallowed. To avoid this, invest the proceeds in a different fund with a different index or strategy.

Do I need to report tax-loss harvesting on my tax return?

Yes. All capital gains and losses are reported on Schedule D and Form 8949. Your broker will issue Form 1099-B showing the proceeds and cost basis of each sale. The forms can be complex, especially for multiple lots — most investors use tax software.

Can tax-loss harvesting reduce my self-employment tax?

No. Capital losses offset capital gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income, but the $3,000 offset is against your adjusted gross income, not specifically against self-employment income. SE tax is calculated on Schedule C net income and is not affected by capital losses.

How do I track my cost basis for tax-loss harvesting?

Most brokers track cost basis automatically and report it on Form 1099-B. If you use specific identification (spec ID), you must designate which shares you are selling at the time of the trade. FIFO (first-in, first-out) is the default method.

Should I harvest losses in December or throughout the year?

Year-round harvesting is generally better than waiting until December. Losses realized earlier in the year give you more flexibility to manage gains and avoid wash-sale issues. However, many investors do most of their harvesting in November and December when they have a clearer picture of their annual gains and losses.

The Bottom Line

Tax-loss harvesting is one of the most effective strategies for reducing your investment tax bill. By intentionally realizing losses on underperforming investments, you can offset capital gains from winning investments and deduct up to $3,000 per year against ordinary income. Unused losses carry forward indefinitely. The strategy is particularly powerful for cryptocurrency investors, who are not subject to wash-sale rules. To maximize the benefit, harvest losses throughout the year (not just in December), track your cost basis carefully, and be mindful of the wash-sale rule for securities. A disciplined tax-loss harvesting strategy can add 0.5% to 1.5% to your annual after-tax returns — a significant boost over a lifetime of investing.
