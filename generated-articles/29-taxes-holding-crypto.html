Key Takeaway

You do not have to pay taxes on cryptocurrency while you hold it without selling, trading, or otherwise disposing of it. Cryptocurrency is treated as property by the IRS, and like stocks or real estate, no tax is due on unrealized gains — the increase in value that exists on paper but has not been converted to cash or other assets. However, there are important exceptions: if you earn crypto through mining, staking, airdrops, or as payment, you may owe tax at the time you receive it even if you continue to hold it. Likewise, if you lend your crypto or participate in DeFi yield protocols, the rewards and interest you earn are taxable as ordinary income in the year received.

Understanding Unrealized vs. Realized Gains

The fundamental tax principle that applies to crypto is the distinction between unrealized gains and realized gains. An unrealized gain is the increase in value of an asset you still own. A realized gain occurs when you sell, trade, spend, or otherwise dispose of the asset. The IRS only taxes realized gains.

This principle is the same for all types of property. When your home increases in value, you do not pay tax on the appreciation until you sell it. When your stocks go up, you do not pay tax until you sell them. The same applies to Bitcoin, Ethereum, and all other cryptocurrencies. Simply holding crypto in your wallet, watching its value increase, does not trigger any tax liability.

When Holding Crypto Still Triggers a Tax Event

While simply holding crypto does not trigger tax, there are several situations where you owe tax even though you have not sold or traded the crypto. These are cases where the IRS considers you to have received income even though you continue to hold the asset:

Mining Rewards

When you successfully mine a block, the crypto rewards are taxable as ordinary income at their fair market value on the date you receive them. This applies to both solo miners and pool participants. The income is reported on Schedule C (business income) or as Other Income on Schedule 1.

Staking Rewards

Staking rewards (proof-of-stake or delegated proof-of-stake) are taxable as ordinary income at the fair market value when you receive them. The IRS has confirmed this treatment in Revenue Ruling 2023-14. This applies even if you never sell the staking rewards and continue to hold them.

Airdrops

Airdropped tokens are taxable as ordinary income at their fair market value when you gain dominion and control over them (typically when you can access and transfer them). If you receive an airdrop and hold it, you still owe tax on the value at receipt.

Crypto as Payment

If you receive crypto as payment for goods or services, it is taxable as ordinary income at its fair market value on the date of receipt, just like cash. If you are self-employed, the value is also subject to self-employment tax. Holding the crypto after receipt does not change the tax treatment.

DeFi Interest/Yield

Interest and yield earned from lending crypto or providing liquidity is taxable as ordinary income when credited. Even if you never withdraw the rewards and keep them in the protocol, the income is taxable in the year it was earned.

Hard Forks

If you held a crypto that underwent a hard fork and you received new tokens (like Bitcoin holders receiving Bitcoin Cash), the new tokens are taxable as ordinary income when you can access them. Simply holding through a fork may trigger income even if you never sell the new tokens.

The Strategic Advantage of Long-Term Holding

One of the most powerful tax benefits of holding crypto without selling is the ability to qualify for long-term capital gains rates. If you hold crypto for more than one year before selling, your gain is taxed at the preferential long-term capital gains rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your taxable income. Short-term gains (held one year or less) are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%.

The Power of Tax Deferral Through Holding

Every year you hold crypto without selling, you defer the tax that would otherwise be due on the gain. This tax deferral is a powerful wealth-building tool. The money that would have been paid in taxes remains invested and can grow tax-free. Over years or decades, the compounding benefit of tax deferral can significantly increase your after-tax returns. This is the same principle that makes tax-advantaged retirement accounts (401(k)s, IRAs) so effective — though with crypto held outside these accounts, the tax is deferred rather than permanently avoided.

Does Holding Crypto in a Retirement Account Change Anything?

Many investors hold crypto within self-directed IRAs or crypto-backed Solo 401(k)s. Inside a tax-advantaged retirement account, the rules are different:

  • Traditional Self-Directed IRA: Holding crypto inside a traditional IRA means all trades, sales, and rebalancing are tax-deferred. You pay no tax on gains while the crypto remains in the IRA. Tax is due only when you withdraw funds in retirement, at ordinary income rates.
  • Roth Self-Directed IRA: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but all growth — including crypto appreciation — is tax-free if you follow the withdrawal rules. You pay no tax on crypto gains inside a Roth IRA, even when you sell.
  • Crypto Solo 401(k): Similar to a self-directed IRA, with higher contribution limits for self-employed individuals. Gains are tax-deferred (traditional) or tax-free (Roth).
  • UBTI Risk: If your IRA generates income from active business activities (like crypto mining or active trading), you may owe Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI) tax. Passive holding and trading generally do not trigger UBTI.

Holding crypto in a retirement account provides significant tax advantages. However, be aware of prohibited transaction rules. Taking physical possession of the crypto or directing the IRA custodian to make specific investments outside the approved structure can disqualify the IRA and trigger immediate taxation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to report my crypto holdings on my tax return?

The IRS requires you to answer a yes/no question on Form 1040 (Schedule 1, Line 27) asking whether you received, sold, exchanged, or otherwise disposed of any digital asset. If you only held crypto and did not engage in any transactions, you answer "No." However, if you received crypto through airdrops, staking, mining, or as payment, you answer "Yes" even if you still hold it.

Is there a wealth tax on crypto holdings?

No. The United States does not have a wealth tax. There is no annual tax based on the value of your crypto holdings. You are only taxed when you realize gains through a sale, trade, or other disposition. Some policymakers have proposed wealth taxes, but none have been enacted at the federal level.

What if my crypto doubled in value but I haven't sold any?

Congratulations — you have an unrealized gain. You do not owe any tax on this gain until you sell, trade, spend, or otherwise dispose of the crypto. You also do not need to report the gain on your tax return (unless you are asked about digital assets on Schedule 1, in which case you answer based on transactions, not holdings).

Do staking rewards create a tax liability even if I don't sell?

Yes. Under IRS Revenue Ruling 2023-14, staking rewards are taxable as ordinary income at their fair market value when you receive them. This applies even if you immediately restake the rewards or never sell them. You must report the value of staking rewards as income in the year received.

Can I avoid tax by never selling my crypto?

If you never sell, trade, or dispose of your crypto, you will never owe capital gains tax on the appreciation. However, if you die while holding crypto, your heirs receive a "step-up in basis" to the fair market value at your death, meaning the built-in gain is permanently eliminated for income tax purposes. This is a powerful estate planning strategy.

Does transferring crypto between wallets trigger a tax event?

No, transferring crypto between your own wallets (hot wallet to cold storage, etc.) is not a taxable event. There is no disposition — you still own the same asset. However, if you transfer crypto to someone else (as a gift, payment, or trade), the transfer may be taxable.

What if I hold crypto that underwent a hard fork?

If you held crypto that underwent a hard fork (e.g., Bitcoin forked to create Bitcoin Cash), the new tokens you receive are taxable as ordinary income when you can access and transfer them. You owe tax on the fair market value of the new tokens even if you never sell them. The IRS addressed this in Revenue Ruling 2019-24.

Do I owe tax on crypto I hold in a foreign exchange?

Holding crypto on a foreign exchange does not change the tax treatment. You owe no tax on unrealized gains regardless of where the crypto is held. However, you may have additional reporting requirements, such as the FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) if the foreign exchange is considered a "financial account" and your aggregate balance exceeds $10,000.

The Bottom Line

Holding cryptocurrency without selling is not a taxable event. You can watch your portfolio grow without worrying about annual tax bills on unrealized gains. This tax-deferred growth is one of the most significant advantages of long-term crypto investing. However, be aware of the exceptions: mining rewards, staking income, airdrops, and crypto received as payment are taxable when received, even if you continue to hold them. For your buy-and-hold positions, the tax comes due only when you sell, trade, or spend — and if you hold for more than one year, you qualify for preferential long-term capital gains rates. The simple act of holding is tax-free, making it one of the most tax-efficient strategies in crypto investing.
