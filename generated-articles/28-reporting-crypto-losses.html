Key Takeaway

You can deduct cryptocurrency losses on your taxes, but the rules are specific. Crypto losses are treated as capital losses and can offset capital gains from other investments. If your total capital losses exceed your capital gains for the year, you can deduct up to $3,000 per year ($1,500 if married filing separately) against ordinary income. Any excess losses beyond $3,000 are carried forward to future tax years indefinitely. To claim a loss, the crypto must have been disposed of (sold, traded, or otherwise transferred) — simply holding a crypto that has declined in value does not create a deductible loss. You must report all crypto transactions, including losses, on Form 8949 and Schedule D.

When Can You Claim a Crypto Loss?

The most important rule for crypto loss deduction is the realization requirement. A loss is only recognized for tax purposes when you dispose of the asset. Simply watching the value of your portfolio decline is not a taxable event. You must sell, trade, spend, or otherwise transfer the crypto to a third party to realize the loss.

The IRS treats cryptocurrencies as property, so the same rules that apply to stocks, bonds, and real estate also apply to crypto. When you dispose of crypto at a loss, the loss is calculated as the difference between your cost basis (what you paid) and the fair market value at the time of disposition. If you received crypto through mining, staking, or as payment, your cost basis is the fair market value at the time you received it.

How to Report Crypto Losses on Form 8949

Reporting crypto losses requires using Form 8949 (Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets), which feeds into Schedule D (Capital Gains and Losses). Each crypto transaction must be listed separately unless you qualify for summary reporting (see below).

1

List Each Transaction

Enter each crypto disposition (sale, trade, spend) on a separate line of Form 8949. Include the date acquired, date sold, proceeds, cost basis, and gain or loss. Use Part I for short-term (held ≤1 year) and Part II for long-term (held >1 year).

2

Calculate Gain or Loss

For each transaction: proceeds minus cost basis. If the result is negative, you have a loss. Enter the loss amount in Column (h) with parentheses. Use the appropriate cost basis method (FIFO, specific identification, etc.).

3

Check the Correct Box

Form 8949 has three columns (A, B, C) each with short-term and long-term sections. Check Box A/C if basis was reported to the IRS, Box B/D if not. Most exchange transactions are now reported on Form 1099-DA, so Box A or C applies.

4

Transfer to Schedule D

Total your short-term gains/losses and long-term gains/losses from Form 8949 and enter them on Schedule D, Part I (line 4) and Part II (line 9). Then combine them on Part III (line 13) for the net capital gain or loss.

5

Apply the Capital Loss Deduction

If your total capital losses exceed gains, enter the excess loss on Schedule D, line 21. The first $3,000 ($1,500 if MFS) offsets ordinary income. The remainder carries forward to future years and must be tracked on the Capital Loss Carryover Worksheet.

6

Attach and File

Attach Form 8949 and Schedule D to your Form 1040. If you have 50+ transactions, you may be eligible to attach a consolidated statement instead of listing each transaction individually. Most crypto tax software generates these forms automatically.

Source: IRS Form 8949 Instructions (2024), IRS Schedule D Instructions (2024), IRS Publication 550.

The $3,000 Annual Limit and Loss Carryforward

One of the most important rules for crypto loss deduction is the $3,000 annual limit on deducting net capital losses against ordinary income. This limit applies to the portion of your net capital loss that exceeds your capital gains for the year. The limit is $1,500 for married taxpayers filing separately.

How the Loss Carryforward Works

If you have a net capital loss of $50,000 in 2025 and $0 in capital gains, you can deduct $3,000 against your ordinary income in 2025. The remaining $47,000 carries forward to 2026. In 2026, if you have $10,000 in capital gains, the carryforward first offsets those gains entirely ($10,000), then you can deduct another $3,000 against ordinary income. The remaining $34,000 carries forward to 2027. This process repeats indefinitely until the loss is exhausted, even if you have no capital gains in a given year (you can still deduct $3,000 per year).

Tracking capital loss carryforwards is your responsibility. The IRS does not track them for you. You must complete the Capital Loss Carryover Worksheet in the Schedule D instructions each year to calculate your remaining carryforward. If you fail to track it properly, you may lose the benefit of future deductions.

Crypto Tax-Loss Harvesting Strategies

Tax-loss harvesting is a strategy where you intentionally sell crypto at a loss to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio. This strategy can reduce your tax bill significantly, but it must be executed carefully to comply with IRS rules.

Offset High-Gain Sales

If you sold a crypto at a large gain earlier in the year, sell losing positions before year-end to offset those gains. This can reduce or eliminate the tax on your winners. Losses offset gains dollar-for-dollar.

Wash Sale Rule (Not Yet Applicable)

Unlike stocks, crypto is not currently subject to wash sale rules. You can sell a crypto at a loss and repurchase the same crypto immediately without disallowing the loss deduction. However, proposed regulations may extend wash sale rules to crypto as early as 2026.

Year-End Harvesting

December is the most common time for tax-loss harvesting. Review your portfolio and identify positions with unrealized losses. Sell them before December 31 to realize the loss for the current tax year. You can repurchase after 30 days to avoid constructive sale issues.

Rebalancing Strategy

Use tax-loss harvesting as an opportunity to rebalance your portfolio. Sell losing positions that no longer fit your investment strategy and reinvest the proceeds in a more suitable asset. The loss provides a tax benefit while improving your portfolio allocation.

Avoid Over-Harvesting

Remember the $3,000 annual limit on deducting losses against ordinary income. If your gains are already zero, harvesting additional losses beyond $3,000 provides no immediate tax benefit — but the losses carry forward. Consider whether the carryforward is worth the transaction costs.

State Tax Considerations

Some states do not conform to federal capital loss treatment. For example, states that piggyback on federal tax law generally follow the same rules, but states without an income tax (Texas, Florida, etc.) provide no state-level benefit for capital losses.

Stolen or Lost Crypto: Casualty and Theft Losses

If your cryptocurrency was stolen in a hack, lost due to an exchange collapse, or sent to the wrong address, you may be able to claim a theft loss or casualty loss. The tax treatment depends on the circumstances:

For personal-use crypto (held as an investment), theft losses are deductible in the year the theft was discovered. The loss is calculated as the lower of your cost basis or the fair market value of the stolen crypto, reduced by any insurance recovery. However, under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), personal casualty and theft losses are only deductible if they are attributable to a federally declared disaster (unless you are in a disaster area). This makes it difficult for most taxpayers to deduct personal crypto theft losses.

For business-use crypto (crypto held by a business or used in a trade), theft losses are generally deductible regardless of disaster status. If you are a cryptocurrency trader (Section 475 election), miner, or business that accepts crypto, theft losses may be fully deductible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I deduct crypto losses if I never sold?

No. Losses must be realized through a sale, trade, or other disposition. Simply holding crypto that has declined in value does not create a deductible loss. You must dispose of the asset to lock in the loss for tax purposes.

What is the difference between short-term and long-term crypto losses?

Short-term losses (held ≤1 year) offset short-term gains first, then long-term gains. Long-term losses (held >1 year) offset long-term gains first, then short-term gains. After offsetting all gains, the remaining loss offsets up to $3,000 of ordinary income.

Do wash sale rules apply to crypto?

Not currently. The wash sale rule (which disallows loss deductions if you repurchase the same security within 30 days) applies to stocks and securities but not cryptocurrencies. However, the IRS has proposed extending wash sale rules to digital assets. Once implemented, crypto wash sales would be disallowed.

Can I deduct losses from a crypto scam?

If you were defrauded in a crypto scam (Ponzi scheme, rug pull, fake investment), you may be able to deduct the loss as a theft loss. The IRS has issued guidance (Revenue Ruling 2009-9) covering Ponzi scheme losses. You will need documentation of the fraud and evidence that you were criminally defrauded.

How do I report crypto losses from an exchange bankruptcy?

If your crypto is stuck on a bankrupt exchange (like FTX or Celsius), you may need to wait until the bankruptcy proceeding resolves to determine the final loss amount. The IRS has not issued specific guidance on crypto bankruptcy losses. Generally, you claim a capital loss when you can determine the amount of the loss with reasonable certainty.

Can I carry forward crypto losses indefinitely?

Yes. There is no expiration on capital loss carryforwards. You can carry forward unused capital losses year after year until they are fully utilized, even if it takes decades. However, you must track the carryforward amount annually using the Capital Loss Carryover Worksheet.

What happens if I don't report crypto losses?

If you do not report crypto losses on your tax return, you forfeit the tax benefit of those losses. While underreporting losses does not trigger penalties (since it does not understate your tax), you lose the opportunity to offset gains and reduce your taxable income. You can amend prior-year returns to claim losses within 3 years of the original filing date.

Is there a minimum loss amount I need to report?

No minimum. Even small losses should be reported. However, for practical purposes, many tax professionals recommend reporting all transactions. If you have a very small loss (under $10), the cost of reporting it may outweigh the benefit, but the IRS requires reporting of all dispositions of virtual currency regardless of gain or loss amount.

The Bottom Line

Reporting crypto losses is a valuable tax strategy that can offset capital gains and reduce your ordinary income by up to $3,000 per year. The key is realizing the loss through a sale or trade — unrealized losses provide no tax benefit. Tax-loss harvesting, careful tracking of carryforwards, and proper documentation of theft or loss events can significantly reduce your tax bill. As the IRS increases crypto enforcement, accurate loss reporting is essential. Use crypto tax software or work with a tax professional to ensure your losses are properly reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D, and carry forward any excess losses to future years.
